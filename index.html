TELUS FLOURISH — Edge AI for Precision Crop Disease Detection

Problem

Monitoring crop health across large fields is slow, inconsistent, and limited in scale. Farmers relying on manual scouting detect disease too late — leading to broad pesticide application, elevated input costs, yield loss, and unnecessary environmental damage.

Approach

  • Captured high-resolution drone imagery of crop fields as the primary data source
  • Built a U-Net convolutional neural network for pixel-level semantic segmentation of diseased crop regions
  • Expanded 588 labeled images to 2,940 using rotation, flipping, brightness variation, and scaling augmentations (Albumentations)
  • Trained with a hybrid Binary Cross Entropy + Dice Loss function to balance classification accuracy and region overlap
  • Applied threshold optimization on validation data to calibrate segmentation mask outputs
  • Converted segmentation outputs into color-coded disease heatmaps (red = high risk, yellow = moderate, green = healthy)
  • Delivered targeted spray zone recommendations and confidence scores through a Streamlit farmer dashboard

Solution

TELUS FLOURISH converts drone imagery into actionable disease intelligence through a multi-stage AI pipeline: image capture → preprocessing + augmentation → U-Net segmentation → heatmap generation → precision treatment recommendation. Farmers receive early disease alerts and targeted spray zones — without applying chemicals across entire fields.

Outcome & Impact

  • 95%+ accuracy on validation segmentation with strong precision, recall, F1, and IoU scores
  • Early disease detection 7–14 days earlier than manual scouting
  • Estimated 15–25% reduction in pesticide usage through precision intervention
  • Potential 5–10% improvement in yield retention through earlier disease control
  • Estimated financial value of $50–$150 per acre per season ($50,000–$150,000 for a 1,000-acre farm)

My Role

Designed and implemented the core computer vision model — a U-Net CNN for semantic segmentation of crop disease in drone imagery. Built the full training pipeline including preprocessing, Albumentations augmentation, hyperparameter tuning with Adam optimizer, and performance evaluation across precision, recall, F1, and IoU metrics. The model is the analytical core of the system, transforming raw drone imagery into pixel-level disease masks that drive all downstream recommendations.

MindBloom — AI-Powered Adaptive Learning for Neurodiverse Students

Problem

Traditional educational systems apply uniform content delivery that doesn't adapt to neurodiverse cognitive profiles — including ADHD, dyslexia, and autism spectrum conditions. One-size-fits-all delivery produces lower comprehension, higher anxiety, and reduced retention for students who learn differently.

Approach

  • Trained a Scikit-learn learner profiling classifier on the UCI Autism Screening Dataset, achieving ~93% accuracy in identifying cognitive profile types
  • Built an LLM + RAG pipeline to dynamically generate personalized lesson content tailored to each learner's classified profile
  • Incorporated OpenNeuro dyslexia research datasets to inform accessibility design decisions
  • Aligned curriculum with EQAO literacy benchmarks for educational validity
  • Applied accessibility-first design: text-to-speech support, dyslexia-friendly interface, and multimodal content delivery
  • Built an adaptive quiz feedback loop and Streamlit prototype for demonstration

Solution

MindBloom combines a machine learning learner profiler with an LLM + RAG adaptive lesson generation pipeline. The system classifies each student's cognitive profile and dynamically generates personalized lesson content, pacing, and format — reducing friction and cognitive load for neurodiverse learners in a fully accessible interface.

Outcome & Impact

  • Learner profiling classifier achieved ~93% accuracy on the UCI Autism Screening Dataset
  • Estimated 40% improvement in reading comprehension through personalized delivery
  • Estimated 30% reduction in learning-related anxiety
  • Estimated 60% increase in retention using multimodal learning

My Role

Designed the AI intelligence layer — the learner profiling classifier and the LLM + RAG adaptive lesson pipeline. Contributed to the adaptive quiz feedback system and supported development of the Streamlit prototype.

AI Strategy

Designing AI Systems Executives Can Trust

Most AI projects fail at the executive presentation, not in the model. When a decision-maker asks "why did the system recommend this?", the answer cannot be "the model said so." Trust requires three things: explainability at the right level of abstraction, confidence scoring that acknowledges uncertainty, and human oversight built into the architecture from the start.

The organizations that succeed with production AI treat trust as an engineering requirement. SHAP values, confidence thresholds, audit trails, and escalation paths are designed in at the same time as the model itself — not bolted on afterward.

Takeaway: explainability is a system design decision, not a post-hoc narrative.

Responsible AI

How to Evaluate AI Beyond Accuracy

Accuracy is a starting point, not a finish line. A model can achieve 95% accuracy and still be unfit for production — if that 5% error is concentrated in specific user groups, edge conditions, or high-stakes scenarios that the validation set didn't cover. I encountered this directly while working on model validation across multiple projects.

Robust AI evaluation asks: who does the model fail? Under what conditions? How does performance degrade as real-world data drifts from training distributions? These require adversarial testing, bias audits, and confidence calibration as mandatory steps — not optional enhancements.

Takeaway: evaluation is an ongoing process, not a single number.

Agentic AI

Agentic AI: When It Helps and When It's Risky

Agentic AI systems — those that take sequences of autonomous actions toward a goal — unlock genuinely new capability. Building Orbi AI as a multi-agent system showed me how powerful this architecture is: specialized agents collaborating to deliver a result none could achieve alone. But autonomy and accountability are in tension.

The right agentic design starts by asking: what is the cost of an incorrect action? Low-cost, reversible decisions are good candidates for agent autonomy. High-stakes actions should require explicit human confirmation built into the loop — not as a limitation, but as a design requirement that makes the system trustworthy enough to actually deploy.

Takeaway: design agentic systems around the consequences of failure, not the ambition of capability.

Career · AI Consulting

Moving from Software Engineering to AI Solutions Consulting

Software engineering teaches you to build systems that work. AI consulting teaches you to build systems that the right people use, trust, and act on. The transition requires more than learning machine learning — it requires developing a second language: moving fluently between technical precision and business consequence.

My path through Bell Canada's enterprise systems, three national hackathons, and applied AI consulting reinforced one consistent lesson: the engineers who make this transition aren't those who abandon their technical depth. They're those who add context — understanding what the outcome is, who the decision-maker is, and what "good enough" means in the organization's specific context.

Takeaway: the most effective AI consultants are engineers who learned to ask "so what?" before "how?"
